Overfishing

What is Overfishing?

Overfishing occurs when marine life is caught at a faster rate than it can be replenished. In other words, it is unsustainable and will cause eventual endangerment of species.

Many governments still continue to subsidize their fleets, allowing unprofitable operations to subsist, and overfishing to occur. increasing fishing efforts over the last 50 years as well as unsustainable fishing practices are pushing many fish stocks to the point of collapse

One particular type of fishing, bottom-trawling, is blamed for some of the worst and unnecessary damage. It involves dropping a large net, around 60 meters-wide in some cases, into the sea and dragging it along with heavy weights from a trawler.
The bycatch, unwanted fish and other ocean life thrown back into the sea, can amount to as much as 90% of a trawl's total catch.

Coral Bleaching

I've never heard of Coral Bleaching. Explain please!

When water is too warm, corals will expel the algae (zooxanthellae) living in their tissues causing the coral to turn completely white.
BUT when a coral bleaches, it is not dead. Corals can survive a bleaching event, but they are under more stress and are subject to mortality.

    Are our coral dying now?

    Nearly all of the world's major coral reef regions (Caribbean/ western Atlantic, eastern Pacific, central and western Pacific, Indian Ocean, Arabian Gulf, Red Sea) experienced some degree of coral bleaching and mortality during the 1980s.

    In 2005, the U.S. lost half of its coral reefs in the Caribbean in one year due to a massive bleaching event. The warm waters centered around the northern Antilles near the Virgin Islands and Puerto Rico expanded southward. Furthermore, in January 2010, cold water temperatures in the Florida Keys caused a coral bleaching event that resulted in some coral death. Water temperatures dropped 12.06 degrees Fahrenheit lower than the typical temperatures observed at this time of year.

    More than 60 coral reef bleaching events out of 105 mass coral moralities were reported between 1979-1990, compared with only three bleaching events among 63 mass coral moralities recorded during the preceding 103 years.

    How can you help?

    Though the main problem that leads to the coral reef bleaching is due to climate change and warmer oceans, there are many thing that you can do to help! Start off with this following list:

  • Support reef-friendly businesses. Ask what your dive shop, boating store, tour operators, hotel and other coastal businesses are doing to save the coral reefs. This is especially important in coastal areas with reefs. Let them know you are an informed consumer and care about reefs.
  • When you visit a coral reef, help keep it healthy by respecting all local guidelines, recommendations, regulations, and customs.
  • Keep it clean. You may be in the habit of picking up your own trash. You may even participate in an organized cleanup.
  • Be an informed consumer. Consider carefully the coral objects that you buy for your coffee table. Ask the store owner or manager from what country the coral is taken and whether or not that country has a management plan to insure that the harvest was legal and sustainable over time.

    Deforestation

    What is Deforestation?

    Deforestation is the permanent destruction of forests in order to make the land available for other uses. While deforestation occurs around the world, tropical rainforests are particularly targeted.

    Forests are cut down for many reasons, but most of them are related to money or to people's need to provide for their families.The biggest driver of deforestation is agriculture. Farmers cut forests to provide more room for planting crops or grazing livestock. Often many small farmers will each clear a few acres to feed their families by cutting down trees and burning them in a process known as "slash and burn" agriculture.

    Deforestation has many negative effects on the environment. The most dramatic impact is a loss of habitat for millions of species. Seventy percent of Earth’s land animals and plants live in forests, and many cannot survive the deforestation that destroys their homes.

    Removing trees deprives the forest of portions of its canopy, which blocks the sun's rays during the day and holds in heat at night. This disruption leads to more extreme temperatures swings that can be harmful to plants and animals.
    Trees also play a critical role in absorbing the greenhouse gases that fuel global warming. Fewer forests means larger amounts of greenhouse gases entering the atmosphere and increased speed and severity of global warming.

    Fracking

    What is Fracking?

    Fracking fluid is born when tons of water is mixed with tons of chemicals including methane, mercury, and lead. Fracking fluid is put to work as it gets pushed through pipelines underground. The fracking fluid is so strong and undergoes so much pressure that it makes big fractures in shale rock. The new cracks in the ground allow natural gas to flow up to the surface where it is collected. Once the fracking fluid’s work is done, it is ready to go back to the wastewater disposal well. While traveling back, some of the fracking fluid loses its way and finds itself into clean groundwater.

    The little fracking fluid that is able to get back to the surface safely is no longer needed and is dumped into a toxic pit that sits out in the sun. Its life comes to an end due to evaporation. The fracking fluid evaporates making its way up to Heaven. On its way up, it harms the atmosphere by making the earth really hot, causing global warming.

    Climate Change

    I hear about climate change, but what it is?

    Though climate change is a natural process, humans and our actions have sped up the process by a tenfold because of our increase in air pollution, use of non-renewable resources, and alteration of biodiversity.

    According to the report, Preparing for a Changing Climate, rising levels of carbon dioxide and other heat-trapping gases in the atmosphere have warmed the Earth and are causing wide-ranging impacts, including rising sea levels; melting snow and ice; more extreme heat events, fires and drought; and more extreme storms, rainfall and floods.

    Read more about Climate Change

    It's effecting the whole world!

    Just taking one example, as temperatures rise around the world, African deserts have been hit hard. With the temperature hotter than ever, there are signs that the hot desert storms have increased the risk of bacterial meningitis in the continent. This is mostly due to the increased exposure to the dust. Many studies have shown that the most outbreaks over the last 8 years occurred after sandstorms or excessively high temperatures.

    According to the US National Academy of Sciences, over the past 30 years, there has been a fourfold increase in the number of large and long-duration forest fires in the American West. The length of the fire season has expanded by 2.5 months, and the size of wildfires has increased several times over. More than half the US Western states have experienced their largest wildfire on record since 2000.
